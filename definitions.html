Definitions

New General Catalogue (NGC)

The New General Catalogue (formally A New General Catalogue of Nebulae and Clusters of Stars, being the Catalogue of the Late Sir John F. W. Herschel, Bart., revised, corrected, and enlarged or NGC, abbreviated) is a catalogue of star clusters, nebulae and galaxies compiled by Danish astronomer Johan Ludvig Emil Dreyer in 1888. Dreyer based the catalogue on earlier work by the British Herschel family, and originally contained 7,840 objects, though he added an additional 5,386 objects in later editions. It is a common reference for astronomers today, though it lacks objects that are only visible with modern equipment.


Apparent Magnitude

The magnitude listed on this site is the apparent magnitude – the brightness as seen by an observer a certain distance from an object. Since apparent magnitude is affected by distance – an object appears brighter the closer you are – two objects emitting/reflecting different amounts of light could appear to be the same brightness.”


Distance (kilo light years)

The distance of objects is measured in kilo light years. A light year is the distance light can travel in a year – 9,460,000,000,000 (9.46 trillion) kilometers. For context, the circumference (distance around) the earth is about 40,070 kilometers, so a light year is roughly equivalent to flying around the earth 236 million times! A kilo light year is 1,000 light years, 9,460,000,000 (9.46 billion) kilometers.


Right Ascension (RA)

Right ascension is similar to longitude, and measures a celestial object’s location along the east/west axis. It begins at the vernal equinox, or the spot that the sun arrives at on the first day of spring, in the constellation Pisces. From there, the east/west circle can be broken down into 24 “hours”, or 15° sections. An object’s RA is measured in hours/minutes according to this system.


Declination (Dec)

o Declination is similar to latitude, and measures a celestial object’s location along the north/south axis. It is measured from the celestial equator and is measured positively above this point and negatively below it. The north celestial pole is then labelled +90°, while the south celestial pole is labelled -90°.


Object Size (Arcminutes, Arcseconds)

The size of these objects is listed in arc minutes and arc seconds. In astronomy, one degree is made up of 60 arc minutes, while an arc minute is made up of 60 arc seconds. This breakdown allows for more accurate measurements.


Constellation

Constellations are commonly recognized groupings of stars in astronomy. They can aid astronomers in finding celestial objects, and are listed to help locate the Messier objects in relation to them.



Object Types

Open Clusters

Open clusters are made up of stars that were probably formed from a nebula around the same time – they’re smaller than globular clusters, with dozens to hundreds of stars, and are usually younger than globular clusters.


Globular Clusters

Globular clusters are near-spherical clusters of stars that formed around the same time. They contain incredibly old stars, and are far larger than open clusters, with hundreds of thousands of stars.


Bright/Diffuse Nebulae

Diffuse nebulae are clouds of gas and dust associated with star formation. Young stars can cause the gas in these nebulae to shine; nebulae like this are called emission nebulae. If the stars aren’t hot enough, the light can reflect off the dust, creating a reflection nebula.


Planetary Nebulae

When a star begins to die, its core, formerly the location of nuclear fusion, collapses to become a white dwarf, and its outer layers are shed, creating a planetary nebula.


Galaxies

Galaxies are systems of stars and other matter that make up the universe. Galaxies can interact gravitationally and merge, and larger galaxies can strip smaller galaxies of stars and interstellar matter. Edwin Hubble first split galaxies into three categories: spiral, elliptical and irregular. Galaxies are incredibly diverse, making these categorizations too simple in some cases.


Double/Binary Stars

Double or binary star systems are systems in which two stars are gravitationally bound and orbit one another. In some systems, the stars are so close they can strip material from one another, while stars in other systems have more distance between them. The double star in the Messier Catalogue is not a true double star system, but two stars that appear to be.
